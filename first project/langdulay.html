GAMABA Awardee (1998)

Lang Dulay

Master T'boli Weaver

Biography

Lang Dulay (August 3, 1928 – April 30, 2015) was a renowned Filipino traditional weaver from Lake Sebu, South Cotabato, and a member of the T’boli people. She began weaving at the age of 12, and over time it became her life’s vocation.

Through her intricate patterns and strict adherence to traditional weaving techniques, she conveyed cultural narratives and helped preserve T’boli heritage. From childhood, she dreamed of establishing a school in her community where people could learn, refine, and perfect their traditional art.

Artistic Contibutions

Lang Dulay was awarded for her perseverance in preserving the T’boli tradition of weaving T’nalak—a refined fabric made from abaca. She mastered a wide range of traditional designs, such as bulinglangit (clouds), bankiring (hair bangs), and kabangi (butterfly), each of which conveyed stories and cultural meanings rather than serving as mere decoration.

Despite the growing commercialization of T’nalak and the increasing preference for modern designs over traditional ones, Dulay remained persistent in practicing the harder, traditional way of weaving. She believed that only through this old process could the fabric truly give voice to the songs and stories passed down by her elders, ensuring that T’boli culture continued to live through her art.

Visual Works

T’nalak is a refined fabric made from abaca fiber. Its production begins with stripping the abaca stem, then carefully processing the fibers into fine threads and drying them. These threads are later handwoven using the traditional methods of the T’boli people, preserving both the craftsmanship and cultural significance of the fabric.
