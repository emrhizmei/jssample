FEATURED ARTISTS

Gawad sa Manlilikha ng Bayan (National Living Treasures Award)

Lang Dulay

Master T'boli Weaver

Lang Dulay (August 3, 1928 – April 30, 2015) was a renowned Filipino traditional weaver from Lake Sebu, South Cotabato, and a member of the T’boli people. She began weaving at the age of 12, and over time it became her life’s vocation. Through her intricate patterns and strict adherence to traditional weaving techniques, she conveyed cultural narratives and helped preserve T’boli heritage. From childhood, she dreamed of establishing a school in her community where people could learn, refine, and perfect their traditional art.

Gawad sa Manlilikha ng Bayan (National Living Treasures Award)

Eduardo Mutuc

Filipino Metalsmith and Sculptor

Eduardo Mutuc was born in October 12, 1949 with nine sibligs in Apalit, Pampanga. He is a known metalsmith who dedicated his life in making religious and secular works in wood, silver, and bronze. Mutuc initially worked as a farmer and later turned to woodcarving as a steady livelihood. However, life did not go as planned, and he earned as little as ₱3.00 a day. A turning point came when he was taught how to do silver plating and Monsignor Fidelis Limcauco commissioned him to create a tabernacle. Through this opportunity, Mutuc discovered his passion for crafting artworks for cathedrals, which would later define his artistic career.

Order of National Artist for Film and Broadcast Arts

Kidlat Tahimik (Eric de Guia)

Father of Philippine Indie Cinema

Kidlat Tahimik (born October 3, 1942) is a Filipino filmmaker from Baguio City, Benguet. He studied at the University of the Philippines, earned an MBA from the Wharton School, and later worked as an economist in Paris. Widely regarded as the “Father of Philippine Independent Cinema,” he was conferred the prestigious Order of National Artist for Film and Broadcast Arts in 2018. Tahimik is known for telling local stories from a distinctly Filipino perspective, creating films that reflect the beauty and realities of Filipino culture while critically examining what he describes as the “neocolonial” condition shaping everyday life.
